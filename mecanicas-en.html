Domination of the blocks

Domination: The control exerted by one block over another(s), preventing them from moving those below them in the same column.

There are different forms of domination:

  1. Single Domination: The control of one or several blocks of the same color in the same column.
  2. Simple Double, Triple, or More Domination: The control of 1, 2, 3, or more blocks.
  3. Composite Domination: The control of 1, 2, 3, or more players simultaneously; when multiple players are affected in the same column.
  4. Mixed Domination: When different types of domination occur in the same column.
  5. Multiple Domination: Refers to different types of domination and their control in different columns, for example, throughout the following figure:

Capture of the blocks

Capture: When one block dominates another(s), it eliminates from the game all those that are in the minority in the same column.

Types of capture:

  1. Sandwich: When a block that is dominated and another of the same color re-dominates the column.
  2. Crushing: When the same player re-dominates the same column.
  3. Mixed: When both types of capture occur simultaneously in the same column.

The special block

Characteristic and importance of the special block

The Special Block: The special block is the most important piece in the game, as it is tasked with reaching the Summit (Top) and winning. It is recommended not to incorporate it at the beginning; typically, once it is introduced, it could be said that the mid-game phase has begun, where all conditions are set to ensure a quick and safe ascent. Moreover, it is a highly defensive block since it is the only one that can restrict the movement of other specials through opposition. Additionally, it is the only block that can be rescued back into the game once captured. It could be said that the special block is the heart of the strategy.

In many cases, it is advantageous to dominate the special block without capturing it until the appropriate moment, as its reintroduction from outside the board in the mid-game or endgame phase can be very perilous.

The opposition of the special

A special block cannot move or even pass over another special block, as they are not allowed to coexist in the same column under any circumstances.

In XIMA, "opposition" refers to the condition where two special blocks are adjacent to each other, separated only by the line or vertex that divides the squares. This situation becomes crucial, particularly during the endgame.

In the position shown in the diagram below, with it being the blue player's turn, if they move their common block to G6 to assist their special block, the red special block could dominate the common block on G6 and snatch victory. On the other hand, if the common block moves to H8, the red special block cannot dominate it because it cannot pass over the blue special block. This last move highlights the opposition between the special blocks, ensuring victory for the blue player.

The rescue of the especial

Only the special block is allowed to reach the top floor or the square (G7); this does not mean that a common block cannot pass over this important square to make a specific move. If your team's special block is captured during the game, then and only then can the affected player move one of their common blocks to the top of the board (G7) and exchange it with the opponent for their special block, which they can reintroduce into the game at their convenience. Of course, the common block must be immediately removed from square (G7).

The blocking

Blocking: It is a structure of blocks that prevents the ascent or descent of other blocks located on different floors or levels. There are different types of blocking:

Upper: This occurs when the structure of blocks that prevents is located above the block attempting to ascend. For example, in this case, the red special block intends to climb to the 3rd floor at square H6, but the blue block impedes its path.

Lower: This occurs when the structure of blocks that prevents passage is located below the block attempting to descend. For example, in this case, if the blue block at H5 were to attempt to dominate the red special block, it would not succeed because the red block at H3, which dominates the blue block, does not allow it. Once it descends to square H4, it loses all its height.

The obstruction

Obstruction: It is a structure of blocks that prevents the movement of another block located on the same floor of the pyramid, regardless of its level.

There are different types of obstruction:

a. Total: This occurs when the obstructed block, in this case the block at E3, has no immediate possibility or hope of reaching square I3 in less than 3 moves, as it would need to go around the entire board to achieve this.

b. Partial: This occurs when the obstructed block can achieve its goal through a different route, though it would require using more moves.

In both cases of blocking and obstruction, the impediment can be internal or external:

Internal: When the block that obstructs the passage is located in the inner ring. External: When the block that obstructs the passage is located in the outer ring.

Value of the blocks and the board

In XIMA, everything has a value, from the blocks to the board itself, which accounts for its profound analysis. The board has a unique and unchangeable value, called the absolute value, determined by the direct position of the blocks on it. However, not all blocks are always directly on the board; they may occupy positions in the three-dimensional space over other blocks, elevating their positional level and continuously modifying the relief. This is referred to as relative value, as these spatial positions change in value depending on the structures formed.

Additionally, blocks, whether on the board or waiting outside to be incorporated, possess a unique and absolute real value, which varies depending on material scarcity or the functions they perform on the board. This constant change also gives them a relative value. All these factors make it practically impossible to determine the true value of a specific block or position in three-dimensional space. Therefore, understanding absolute value in a game is crucial for achieving victory.

Absolute Value of the Blocks

All blocks in XIMA are equal and have the same absolute value of one point when they are outside the board. Unlike common blocks, the special block always has double the value wherever it is located. This is because, in addition to having the same dynamic movement possibilities, it has the responsibility to reach the Top, its opposition capability, and the possibility of being rescued.

Once incorporated into the game, blocks are valued based on the floor they are supported on, even when dominating other blocks. To determine their value, one simply observes which floor these dominated blocks are supported on. For example:

  • Blocks directly supported or supported on other blocks located on the first floor are valued at 2 points.
  • On the second floor, blocks are valued at 4 points.
  • On the third floor, blocks are valued at 6 points.
  • On the fourth floor, blocks are valued at 8 points.
  • For the special block, its value is considered infinite on the fourth floor.

Relative value of the blocks

Another value that must be considered is when one block dominates another(s). The dominant block acquires half the value of all the dominated block(s). For example, if a block dominates another on the first floor, the dominant block gains a total value of 3 points because its dynamic potential increases from that position, while 1 point is subtracted from the value of the dominated block because its mobility is limited. However, its value does not disappear entirely, as it still serves functions, such as preventing the dominant block from leaving that position and slightly limiting its movement decisions, as it would eventually release it.

As shown in the following example, on the third floor, for the blocks to the left of the top, the dominant block acquires a total value of 9 points, while the dominated block retains 3 points. In the case of the blocks to the right, notice how the special block acquires 6 points—half the value of each block it dominates—plus 12 points for being supported on blocks from the third floor, totaling 18 points.

Capturing a block can be worth as many points as its current position or even more, depending on the functions it performs, the consequences it triggers, or the effort required to reach that position. To calculate this, assign a real value to each of these functions, such as incorporating, aligning, cardinal moves, semi-coordinating, dominating, capturing, obstructing, blocking, pinning, reserving, managing, doubling, diverting, simplifying, threatening, counterattacking, creating a ladder, dis-coordinating, isolating, freeing, grouping, synchronizing, recovering, sacrificing, occupying, pressuring, forcing, semi-blocking, timing moves, ascending, descending, etc. Then, approximate what might be going through your opponent's mind and calculate whether a move is good or which one is the best and most profitable.

Scoring System called CIMAX

The CIMAX scoring system is a mathematical method, based on statistical calculation, to measure the level and relative skill of players in XIMA. This method is highly accurate because it does not solely consider whether a player wins or loses a match to draw conclusions. It also takes into account factors such as the strength of moves, power, level of play, time used in relation to the agreed time, and the number of turns taken. This provides a more realistic representation of the conditions under which the match was played.

This system ensures that the outcome of a match is judged directly and in real-time, without relying on an expected result based on the difference in level between players. It is fairer and more precise to draw conclusions from existing statistical data and then combine this result with the level difference to establish a ranking.

Below, we will present the formulas and their application for establishing the CIMAX ranking under any circumstances that may arise between players. It is important to note that the CIMAX points a player wins are subtracted from the CIMAX points of the player who loses. The system functions somewhat like cryptocurrency, where knowledge is gained or lost based on the experience of the participating players. When a new player joins, they are assigned 1000 CIMAX points to start.

In case you're wondering how, even when a player loses in XIMA, they still gain experience and knowledge—don’t worry, the same happens to the winner. The only way to validate this is through the result of the next match.

Below is the formula used to calculate CIMAX, explained in the most user-friendly way possible. While we acknowledge that this content is dense, it is necessary and forms an integral part of XIMA’s creation and its innovative contributions.

\text{CIMAX}=\left( \text{Level of Play} \right)
    \left( \dfrac{\text{Maximum Game Power}+1}{\text{Minimum Game Power}+1} \right)
    \left( \dfrac{\text{Loser's CIMAX}}{\text{Winner's CIMAX}} \right)

In other words: The new CIMAX is equal to the level of play multiplied by the result of the division between the player with the highest power plus one and the player with the lowest power plus one. This is then multiplied by the result of the division between the current CIMAX of the losing player and that of the winning player.

To calculate the CIMAX, the first thing you need to know is the level of play, which is simply the sum of the playing strengths of both players.

\quad \quad \quad \quad\quad \quad\quad \quad
  \quad \quad \quad \quad
 L_G= F_G(1)+F_G(2),

where and are the game force of the first and second player respectively.

How to calculate game force was previously explained in order to understand the average value of the blocks so that you could get an idea of that specific position in a match that hadn’t finished yet. The truth is, it doesn’t differ much from what I will explain next, but now we will show you the complete formula, even for cases where the game ends, and when a player resigns or runs out of time.

\quad \quad \quad \quad\quad \quad\quad \quad
  \quad \quad 
F_G = V_{BB} + V_{OB} + V_{CB} + V_R+V_W,

where:

  • (Value of the blocks on the board): We explained how to calculate it in Sec. ( ).
  • (Value of the blocks off the board): Once the first block is incorporated, the value of each block off the board equals the average value of the blocks on the board.
  • (Value of the captured blocks): Before a block is captured, it always has a value in that position. When captured, it retains that last value, which is added as force points to the player who captured it.
  • (Resignation value): The player who resigns will give the opposing player half of their game force. If a player runs out of time, it is also considered a resignation.
  • (Win value): When the special block is positioned at the top, the game is automatically won, and a value of 16 XIMAs is added to the game force. Normally, a common block would generate 8 XIMAs, but remember that this one is special.

Game power is directly proportional to game force and inversely proportional to the time on the clock and the number of turns used by the player. Having a higher game power increases the game force and raises the likelihood of victory, but does not guarantee it due to possible mistakes ("bad moves") that the player with greater power might make, which led us to create the following formula:

P_G = \frac{F_G}{2^{te} \cdot T},

Threats to the special

In the following case, the blue special block can be dominated by the red blocks. In this example, it is threatened by three blocks simultaneously: (E3, I6, I7). We will now show the different known possible ways to evade this threat:

  • Moving to a non-attacked place, either on the same level taking advantage of the board’s features, or to a higher or lower level. In this case, it could move to (J4, H2, or E7), escaping all threats.
  • Dominating with the special block a block of our own or an opponent’s, or even capturing the attacking block if applicable. For example, if we dominate with the special block any of these blocks (E3, I6, I7), we would solve the problem. However, if we dominate with the special block (H4 or K5), we can also protect it by dominating with the block at (I4) and moving it to (I3), where we would achieve a solution, though it would be less effective but still valid.
  • Blocking or obstructing the attacking blocks. For instance, if we dominate the block at I6 with the block at G6, we obstruct the block at I7, but not the block at E3.

    • Deflecting or recapturing could be another alternative. For example, if we move the block from I4 to H4, note how if our special block is dominated, we could capture that block.

  • Other temporary, more indirect alternatives would be to counter-attack the opponent’s special block to gain time. For example, if we move KJ4, and lastly, the most desperate solution would be to threaten the top with a common block, like LG6, to indicate that if the special block is captured, it could be immediately rescued and repositioned in a better place.

His mastery and precision in this phase of the game are universally recognized and constitute a source of study for anyone who aims to achieve a comprehensive understanding of this phase of the game. As you can see, his knowledge is not only universal but also perfectly applicable to other genres like XIMA.

Regarding endgame study, Capablanca says in his book "Últimas Lecciones" (written in 1942):

"To study and practice Chess, it is advisable to divide the game into three parts: the opening, the middle game, and the endgame. These three parts are closely interconnected, and it would be a grave error to study the opening without considering the middle game and the endgame. Likewise, it would be an error to study the middle game without considering the endgame.

The above reasoning clearly demonstrates that to perfect oneself in XIMA, the endgame must be studied first, as the endgame can be studied and learned on its own, while the middle game and the opening should only be studied in relation to the endgame.
