What is XIMA?

XIMA is the closest material representation of our complex and incredible way of thinking.

Human nature pushes us to aim for the top from the very beginning, even as sperm, where a tough competition starts to reach the ultimate goal: the start of life.

XIMA was created to teach people how to think and find happiness. It began with a promise of love—to make the best board game ever, bringing families together to share meaningful moments.

From the start, the goal was clear: to create something truly unique. This meant breaking away from flat, two-dimensional designs and adding a new dimension to board games. We were determined to achieve this, even if it meant challenging conventional ideas. Instead of copying existing game mechanics, we committed to building something entirely original.

Our ambition to create the most complete game in history required years of thought before we designed the first prototype. This wasn’t just another game—there were already plenty of those. XIMA had to stand out: unique, original, diverse, and strategically advanced. We set a bold goal: to surpass even classics like Chess and Go. After 12 years, XIMA became a reality.

XIMA combines a three-dimensional design with deep strategy and tactics. While it doesn’t defy gravity, the game uses it as a core element. We’ve even added a fourth dimension for professional players. Its hybrid style adapts to all kinds of players, making it the first of its kind. With a third degree of movement freedom, XIMA introduces players to a world of science and strategy. It has the highest known combinatorial complexity—1.7 billion possibilities by the second turn for two players. Yet, despite its depth, XIMA is easy to learn and always guarantees a winner.

Here are the unique features that set XIMA apart from other games:

  • Three Dimensions: XIMA’s four-level, staggered 3D board allows movement across levels and in two dimensions. Professional games may even include teleportation for added strategic depth.
  • Unprecedented Combinations: By the second turn, XIMA offers over 1.7 billion possible moves and combinations, even with just two players. This doesn’t include teleportation or the variations with triangular, hexagonal, or circular boards.
  • Easy Learning Curve: Despite its complexity, XIMA is easy to learn. Within 5-10 minutes, players are ready to start. All blocks move in the same way, and only vertical movement between floors requires attention.
  • New Gameplay Concept: XIMA introduces a hybrid genre called Scienluck where players can choose to include chance (like dice games) or play purely strategically (like chess). This new concept balances the game for players of all skill levels.
  • Inclusive Play: XIMA allows more than two players at once, creating new ways to compete while staying true to its core objectives. It also encourages couples, teams, or mixed-gender play, fostering diversity.
  • Peaceful Objective: Unlike many games, XIMA promotes victory through building strategies rather than destruction. It goes beyond social barriers, focusing on personal growth and positive competition.
  • No Draws: Designed to avoid the draws common in chess or Go, XIMA always guarantees a winner by encouraging continuous material growth and evolving strategies.
  • Simplified Notation: XIMA uses an easy-to-understand notation system that makes it more accessible to study and analyze.
  • Accessibility: XIMA’s simple design makes it easy to produce and transport, with uniform cube blocks and foldable boards. This accessibility encourages its spread to economically challenged or remote areas.

This book opens the door to a new universe. Although XIMA is new and original, we know there is still a long journey ahead. Its teaching methodology is just beginning, offering a path to potential greatness for those who help its tactical, technical, and strategic development.

Join us in this journey of learning and growth, where everyone can develop their skills through the knowledge and practice of XIMA.

Characterization and values of XIMA

XIMA, like other board games, represents a static space where dynamic pieces, or "blocks," move, similar to how the material world changes over time.

In XIMA, the main goal is to reach the highest position on square G7. This makes positioning the most important part of the game. Players must plan the best strategies to reach this shared goal, often using the special block to move up.

XIMA is one of the few games in the world that can be played without depending on luck. Both players start equally, but the game can also include dice to make it more fun and accessible, perfect for family and friends. Because of this, XIMA doesn’t fit into traditional game types like "Chance" or "Science" but combines both.

This mix of strategy and flexibility makes XIMA part of a new genre—Scienluck, which combines science and chance.

The Scienluck Mode

Scienluck mode was created to give all players a fair chance to win, regardless of their opponent's skill, and to make losing less discouraging.

Scienluck is a mix between strategy (science) and luck. Players can either roll two dice on their turn and make two moves (one for each die), or they can make one safe and legal move with any of their blocks—this is called the "science" or "skill" mode.

To understand Scienluck, let's first summarize the two main types of board games:

  • Games of Chance: These games rely heavily on luck. Players don’t need much preparation because luck can change the outcome, no matter how predictable things seem.
  • Games of Skill: These are tactical, strategic games with no luck involved. Players win by using observation, preparation, and study. Both players start equally.
  • Scienluck Games: A hybrid of luck and skill, where both players have equal chances depending on their decisions. It's semi-predictable but still requires strategy and tactics.

When we developed Scienluck mode, we first studied how to modify the original game. We wanted to ensure both players—whether using luck (dice) or skill (no dice)—had equal chances. To balance things, we allowed the player using dice to make two moves if the dice added up to 12 (the highest sum), giving them access to any part of the board. This player could also move two different blocks with the dice results, such as using a 5 to bring in a block from outside and a 3 to move another block already on the board.

This system allows flexibility: the player can use one block to move in two different directions or use the dice separately. For example, they could move 3 steps in one direction and then 5 in another. But, of course, they must follow all the XIMA rules.

We believe this balance works well, though we are constantly improving the game. We're also considering allowing dice results to be subtracted for additional movement options.

Scienluck reflects a philosophical balance between materialism and idealism, as it tests which side—luck or skill—ultimately prevails.

Scienluck Solo Play

Scienluck also offers a great solo mode, where you can play against yourself. By choosing the "science" side, you can focus entirely on strategy, while the "chance" side adds unpredictability with the dice rolls. This keeps the game dynamic, as you never know the outcome.

For those who have played chess against themselves, you know how difficult it can be to win or lose since you already know your own plans. You end up calculating every move in depth, which can lead to mistakes. This is why we believe XIMA is excellent for solo play—it mirrors real life with its complex, ever-changing possibilities.

XIMA is a game that reflects the personality of each player. Through it, you learn much about yourself and others: whether you're strategic, quick-thinking, honest, polite, or competitive. Most importantly, it helps you learn from your mistakes, growing as both a player and a person.

XIMA also improves decision-making in complex situations, teaching you to filter out unnecessary information and focus on what's important. It strengthens logical thinking, imagination, attention, and concentration.

Origins of XIMA

XIMA was conceived in 2010 in Havana, Cuba, by Yosdel Vicente Muiño Acevedo and Dr. Liz Power Suarez. The idea emerged after writing a children's story called The Pawn King.

The story is set in a magical chess world where the pieces come to life. A simple pawn dreams of marrying a beautiful lady. While writing, I avoided focusing too much on chess and let my imagination run free, wondering what it would be like to invent a completely new game.

This dream became so vivid that it felt real. Then, my son Ledsoy was born, named after the hero of the story, with his name being my own reversed. Later, my daughter Caissa was born, named after the muse of chess, though I didn’t know it at the time.

The reinvention of chess became so detailed in my mind that I felt ready to create something entirely new—a promise I made to my partner to invent the next great board game.

We decided that the new game should move upward, even if it meant defying gravity. The concept of the pyramid inspired us to begin developing the rules, and together, we created XIMA.

Rules of XIMA

Main Objective of the Game:

• Bring your special block to the top of the board before your opponents.

Basic Rules:

  1. At the start of the game, all blocks (10 in total) are outside the board.
  2. Each player takes one turn and can either bring a new block onto the board or move an already placed block.
  3. Blocks move in straight lines in any direction—vertical, horizontal, or diagonal—for as many steps as you like. If playing with chance, they move the number of steps shown by the dice.
  4. When bringing a block onto the board, both the first and last steps must be on the board.
  5. Once a block is on the board, it cannot move off the board.
  6. Blocks can go up or down on their next move to any spot with the same height or lower than where they are.
  7. Moving a block over another block stops its movement and eliminates any blocks beneath it that are a different color in the same column.
  8. If a special block is captured, the player can bring a regular block to the top of the board to rescue the special block, which can be placed back on the board later.
  9. A regular block can only go to the top to rescue the special block; otherwise, it can pass over square G7.
  10. A player loses if they have no blocks left on or off the board when it's their turn.
  11. A player wins if they control all of their blocks on the board.
  12. A player cannot repeat the same position or sequence of moves for three turns in a row.
  13. If the game reaches a deadlock or draw, each player gets three extra blocks, known as a "material increase," instead of ending in a draw.

At first, the rules were much simpler; there were barely three. The first rule was about how to go up. I remember thinking, "But how do you go up?" At that point, there wasn't even a physical board, just drawings on paper. Luckily, Liz was there to help me figure it out. Then, we gave movement to the blocks. We decided to call the pieces "blocks" because pyramids are built from blocks, and we wanted to avoid confusion with the names used in other games.

For the movement, we first tested with Chess pieces before deciding on blocks. The game played very well, and even the knight could move up from one level to another without using a step, but it couldn't win the game. This was good, but it wasn't the solution we needed. At that time, we called the game "Cima," in Spanish, which means top or summit, and it could be played with Chess-like pieces in cube form. This idea came from an earlier project I created at university called "Hustle," which later led to "360 degrees"—the predecessor of XIMA. In "360 degrees," players moved from the center of the board, controlling squares based on dice rolls. One die showed cardinal directions (north, south, east, west, and diagonals), and the other showed how many steps to move. This concept influenced the movement of the blocks in XIMA.

Of course, I knew the goal was to reach the top, but something was still missing in the gameplay. I was tempted to follow the trend of creating animated blocks to simulate war, aggression, and destruction. This would have been popular and profitable, but I had the courage to create a game with a noble, peaceful objective. The blocks would simply combine to reach the top and win, without destroying anything. This was our message to future generations.

In avoiding the temptation to follow certain design trends, we made sure XIMA still resembled traditional games, without losing its modern, original essence. If you notice similarities to games like Parcheesi, with its goal of reaching the center, or Checkers and Chinese Checkers with pieces jumping over others, that’s no accident. XIMA also uses a notation system like Chess, has blocks outside the board like the stones in Go, and incorporates many familiar elements. Everything was well thought out and calculated, guided by a dream and faith.

I won't spend time on details about how it all came together, so let’s move on to learn more about XIMA. The depth I share with you is just the surface of this beautiful universe that is waiting to be discovered. Of course, we risk being wrong, so I encourage you to question everything and challenge any methodology or theory I present. This way, XIMA can evolve as quickly as possible.

The XIMA Board: Levels and Rings

The XIMA board, or pyramid, has a stepped, three-dimensional shape with 4 levels. When viewed from the top, it has 13 rows of squares vertically and 13 rows of squares horizontally, for a total of 169 squares, alternating between light and dark colors (white and black). The pyramid is placed so that one face is directly in front of the players.

From a top-down view, here are the positions that players should occupy based on the number of participants. The shaded area in red represents one of the four pyramid faces: (A1-M1-G7); (M1-M13-G7); (M13-A13-G7); (A13-A1-G7).

From a side view of the board, we can see that it has 4 levels, with the 4th being the highest and the most important since the first one to move their special block to this level will be the champion of the game.

Each level, except for the fourth, is composed of two rings: the inner ring indicated on the first level, which is the largest and colored orange, and the outer ring indicated in blue on the second level, which is the medium one.

The area shaded in yellow is called a band and refers to two adjacent rows, in this case, vertical, that are on the same face on a given level. The squares A1, M1, M13, A13 are individually called outer corner squares, and the squares B2, L2, L12, B12 are called inner corner squares, similarly on each level. From a top view, if we draw an imaginary line from G1 to G13 and from A7 to M7, these squares together would be called central squares. The squares G1, M7, M13, A7 are individually called outer central squares, and the squares G2, L7, G12, B7 are called inner central squares, similarly on each level. From a top view, if we draw an imaginary line from A1 to M13 and from M1 to A13, these squares together would be called upper diagonals.

Each level has a height of two steps, and each step has the height of one block. The 4th level can also be referred to as G7 or Summit.

Position and movement of the blocks

At the start of the game, the blocks are outside the board, each player has a total of 10, including one special block that will be responsible for reaching the summit or G7. All blocks move the same way and can pass through any square on the board but always in a straight line from their current position, whether vertical, horizontal, diagonal, or at 45 and 90-degree angles up and down relative to the support plane, in an unlimited number of steps or as indicated by the dice when playing in chance mode.

Its movement can only be stopped by a block structure or by the board's own limitations. The player can introduce a new block on their turn or, if they prefer, move one that is already on the board. Once a block is on the board, it can never leave the game until the end of the match or until it is captured.

Technically, to introduce a new block onto the board, it must be done as follows: in a straight line forward on the face where it is to be introduced (from outside to inside), right diagonal, or left diagonal. Example:

Important: when a block is introduced, it must do so onto the board; it cannot start or end its movement on top of another block, even of its own color. Otherwise, the game would not make sense, as the second player would move on top of the first player's block each time a block is introduced. Blocks can ascend to any position on their next step as long as their ceilings are together and aligned. And they can descend to any position.

The notation system

The notation system used in XIMA originates from an adaptation of the system used in Chess, known as simplified algebraic notation. However, in XIMA, we call it very simplified algebraic notation. Since we have only one type of movement for the 10 blocks, unlike Chess, which uses 6 pieces with different movements, it is unnecessary in XIMA to specify the names of the blocks unless referring to the moment of incorporating the special block, which is denoted by an equal sign (=). Another difference is that in XIMA, the board uses uppercase letters to define rows and columns, as they do not interfere with the names of any blocks.

The very simplified algebraic notation system has two modes: the long or complete mode, which is easier for beginners to learn, and the short or abbreviated mode, which is more efficient for advanced players.

In the long mode, you simply write the name of the square from which the block starts, followed by the name of the square it reaches. This method is straightforward and ideal for beginners.

In the short mode, typically used by experienced players, only the square where the block arrives is mentioned, without specifying the starting square most of the time. This approach is less cumbersome to annotate and easy to understand once skills in XIMA are acquired.

Below, we provide examples of how to record moves in both modes for better understanding.

  1. a) - Move number followed by a period.
  2. b) - Name of the destination square of the moving block. (In the long mode, the starting square is indicated followed by the destination square).

    3. Note: If a new block is introduced onto the board, a hyphen or minus sign (-) must precede the move, and if it is the special block, an equal sign (=).

Outcome of a Match

A game ends when:

  • A player reaches the top with a special block in a legal manner.
  • An opponent voluntarily abandons the game.
  • The clock is used and the opponent exceeds the time limit for reflection.
  • All blocks belonging to a single player are captured.

It is very common for players not to finish games, as one opponent may find it unnecessary to continue trying to stop the other from climbing to victory and will abandon the game at their convenience. It is considered unethical to keep playing from an inferior position against a recognized strong opponent, as the outcome is seen as inevitable.

However, in professional XIMA matches, where statistics such as strength and playing power are recorded, it is recommended to play until the end. A defeat does not define a player's level of skill but instead demonstrates the determination to fight until the very last moment with dignity, which is ultimately the essence of life. Additionally, the audience always appreciates witnessing the game being played out to its conclusion.

The drowned

When a player cannot move any block on their turn, they are declared "stalemate" and lose the game. This rule is necessary to always give the opponent a chance to defend themselves. It is a gesture of honor and hope that is very important and can be useful in games with more than two participants.

Without Resource

When a player cannot perform any function in the game due to the lack of all their blocks, they will lose the game.

Increasing of material or blocks

As we all know, XIMA does not allow draws, so any situation that leads to this outcome will always resort to material increment for all players equally.

Material increment involves adding an additional 3 blocks to the game, on top of the initial 10, and is only resorted to under the following circumstances:

  1. The position reached is such that no player can reach the top (G7) with the special block within a series of legal moves. This is referred to as a "dead position," provided that the move leading to this position was legal.
  2. By mutual agreement proposed by any of the players, where all players decide unanimously on the increment.
  3. The game can be considered for increment if an identical position is about to appear or has appeared on the board at least 4 times.
  4. The game can be considered for increment if, in the last 50 consecutive moves of each player, no captures occurred between blocks or no player ascended to a higher level or floor.

Domination of the blocks

Domination: The control exerted by one block over another(s), preventing them from moving those below them in the same column.

There are different forms of domination:

  1. Single Domination: The control of one or several blocks of the same color in the same column.
  2. Simple Double, Triple, or More Domination: The control of 1, 2, 3, or more blocks.
  3. Composite Domination: The control of 1, 2, 3, or more players simultaneously; when multiple players are affected in the same column.
  4. Mixed Domination: When different types of domination occur in the same column.

Capture of the blocks

Capture: When one block dominates another(s), it eliminates from the game all those that are in the minority in the same column.

Types of capture:

  1. Sandwich: When a block that is dominated and another of the same color re-dominates the column.
  2. Crushing: When the same player re-dominates the same column.
  3. Mixed: When both types of capture occur simultaneously in the same column.

The special block

Characteristic and importance of the special block

The Special Block: The special block is the most important piece in the game, as it is tasked with reaching the Summit (Top) and winning. It is recommended not to incorporate it at the beginning; typically, once it is introduced, it could be said that the mid-game phase has begun, where all conditions are set to ensure a quick and safe ascent. Moreover, it is a highly defensive block since it is the only one that can restrict the movement of other specials through opposition. Additionally, it is the only block that can be rescued back into the game once captured. It could be said that the special block is the heart of the strategy.

In many cases, it is advantageous to dominate the special block without capturing it until the appropriate moment, as its reintroduction from outside the board in the mid-game or endgame phase can be very perilous.

The opposition of the special

A special block cannot move or even pass over another special block, as they are not allowed to coexist in the same column under any circumstances.

In XIMA, "opposition" refers to the condition where two special blocks are adjacent to each other, separated only by the line or vertex that divides the squares. This situation becomes crucial, particularly during the endgame.

In the position shown in the diagram below, with it being the blue player's turn, if they move their common block to G6 to assist their special block, the red special block could dominate the common block on G6 and snatch victory. On the other hand, if the common block moves to H8, the red special block cannot dominate it because it cannot pass over the blue special block. This last move highlights the opposition between the special blocks, ensuring victory for the blue player.

The rescue of the especial

Only the special block is allowed to reach the top floor or the square (G7); this does not mean that a common block cannot pass over this important square to make a specific move. If your team's special block is captured during the game, then and only then can the affected player move one of their common blocks to the top of the board (G7) and exchange it with the opponent for their special block, which they can reintroduce into the game at their convenience. Of course, the common block must be immediately removed from square (G7).

The blocking

Blocking: It is a structure of blocks that prevents the ascent or descent of other blocks located on different floors or levels. There are different types of blocking:

Upper: This occurs when the structure of blocks that prevents is located above the block attempting to ascend. For example, in this case, the red special block intends to climb to the 3rd floor at square H6, but the blue block impedes its path.

Lower: This occurs when the structure of blocks that prevents passage is located below the block attempting to descend. For example, in this case, if the blue block at H5 were to attempt to dominate the red special block, it would not succeed because the red block at H3, which dominates the blue block, does not allow it. Once it descends to square H4, it loses all its height.

The obstruction

Obstruction: It is a structure of blocks that prevents the movement of another block located on the same floor of the pyramid, regardless of its level.

There are different types of obstruction:

a. Total: This occurs when the obstructed block, in this case the block at E3, has no immediate possibility or hope of reaching square I3 in less than 3 moves, as it would need to go around the entire board to achieve this.

b. Partial: This occurs when the obstructed block can achieve its goal through a different route, though it would require using more moves.

In both cases of blocking and obstruction, the impediment can be internal or external:

Internal: When the block that obstructs the passage is located in the inner ring. External: When the block that obstructs the passage is located in the outer ring.

Value of the blocks and the board

In XIMA, everything has a value, from the blocks to the board itself, which accounts for its profound analysis. The board has a unique and unchangeable value, called the absolute value, determined by the direct position of the blocks on it. However, not all blocks are always directly on the board; they may occupy positions in the three-dimensional space over other blocks, elevating their positional level and continuously modifying the relief. This is referred to as relative value, as these spatial positions change in value depending on the structures formed.

Additionally, blocks, whether on the board or waiting outside to be incorporated, possess a unique and absolute real value, which varies depending on material scarcity or the functions they perform on the board. This constant change also gives them a relative value. All these factors make it practically impossible to determine the true value of a specific block or position in three-dimensional space. Therefore, understanding absolute value in a game is crucial for achieving victory.

Absolute Value of the Blocks

All blocks in XIMA are equal and have the same absolute value of one point when they are outside the board. Unlike common blocks, the special block always has double the value wherever it is located. This is because, in addition to having the same dynamic movement possibilities, it has the responsibility to reach the Top, its opposition capability, and the possibility of being rescued.

Once incorporated into the game, blocks are valued based on the floor they are supported on, even when dominating other blocks. To determine their value, one simply observes which floor these dominated blocks are supported on. For example:

  • Blocks directly supported or supported on other blocks located on the first floor are valued at 2 points.
  • On the second floor, blocks are valued at 4 points.
  • On the third floor, blocks are valued at 6 points.
  • On the fourth floor, blocks are valued at 8 points.
  • For the special block, its value is considered infinite on the fourth floor.

Relative value of the blocks

Another value that must be considered is when one block dominates another(s). The dominant block acquires half the value of all the dominated block(s). Scoring System called CIMAX

The CIMAX scoring system is a mathematical method, based on statistical calculation, to measure the level and relative skill of players in XIMA. This method is highly accurate because it does not solely consider whether a player wins or loses a match to draw conclusions. It also takes into account factors such as the strength of moves, power, level of play, time used in relation to the agreed time, and the number of turns taken. This provides a more realistic representation of the conditions under which the match was played.

This system ensures that the outcome of a match is judged directly and in real-time, without relying on an expected result based on the difference in level between players. It is fairer and more precise to draw conclusions from existing statistical data and then combine this result with the level difference to establish a ranking.

Below, we will present the formulas and their application for establishing the CIMAX ranking under any circumstances that may arise between players. It is important to note that the CIMAX points a player wins are subtracted from the CIMAX points of the player who loses. The system functions somewhat like cryptocurrency, where knowledge is gained or lost based on the experience of the participating players. When a new player joins, they are assigned 1000 CIMAX points to start.

In case you're wondering how, even when a player loses in XIMA, they still gain experience and knowledge—don’t worry, the same happens to the winner. The only way to validate this is through the result of the next match.

\text{CIMAX}=\left( \text{Level of Play} \right)

Zil Maneuver

This maneuver is named after the creator of XIMA, Dr. Liz Power Suarez, whose name is reversed as "Zil Power" in this context. She applied this maneuver in a tournament game and won automatically.

This maneuver is present in the middlegame of the game, covering the first three levels of the board, connecting them with each other. This maneuver has 4 phases:

The first phase: Place a block on the third level. It can be on any square of the third level, but it is recommended to be on the outer squares, primarily on the corner squares, as they also protect the special block during its ascent.

The second phase: Align and semi-coordinate a block from the interior square of the second level with the block on the third level. Semi-coordinate: This means that two blocks are aligned, but only one can move on top of the other. In this case, the block on the third level can move on top of the block on the second level, thereby defending against anyone dominating the block on the second level, as it is ready to capture.

The third phase: Create a ladder block on the first level that allows alignment and coordination of the block being incorporated on more than one square of the second level. Generally, the best position is in the central inner square of the first level. Coordinate: This means that at least two aligned blocks can move on top of each other.

The fourth phase: Evade any blockades that the opponent might provide and incorporate the block into a square that will subsequently allow you to dominate the ladder block on the second level.

Then, it is just a matter of applying the appropriate maneuver depending on the position to finally reach the top.

What is the Objective of Openings in XIMA?

The main and primary objective of any good opening is to position the blocks to respond to the opponent's actions and ascend as quickly as possible. Ineffective moves that do not contribute to development, repeated moves of the same block that must retreat from the opponent's attacks, or moves that shift a block from square to square without finding a satisfactory position, as well as premature attacks with insufficient material that only result in a loss of time, should be avoided. However, as with everything in XIMA, nothing is absolute.

All openings aim to fulfill these conditions while preventing the opponent from doing the same. In the opening, the first player in XIMA has the initiative, while the second player has positional and material advantages. If the first player wastes time, it can lead to disastrous consequences and may become a point of no return.

The way a XIMA game begins conditions its entire development. Ignorance of the principles and mechanisms of the opening can lead an inexperienced player into lost positions within the first few moves. The possible combinations of moves are so vast that a minor error can be significantly amplified in the subsequent move.

In particular, avoid wasting time capturing an innocuous block at the start; in the opening, two moves are often worth more than a block. Do not sacrifice material without reason, but do not hesitate to invest a block in the opening if it results in good compensation, such as:

  • A significant advantage in development.
  • The possibility of opening ascent lines.
  • Placing a block on the third floor.

Designate one or two stair-step blocks, but no more. Stair-step blocks are blocks that are destined not to ascend. Develop your forces with the objective of occupying and/or controlling the second floor, then the third, and so on, until victory is achieved.

  • Complete development before maneuvering for an attack (however, there are many exceptions to this rule).
  • Avoid "hunting" blocks (capturing or blocking) in the early moves.

So far, only one efficient maneuver is known that allows us to get a block to a higher level before the opponent; this maneuver is the Feedback Maneuver.

The Feedback Maneuver

The Feedback Maneuver is present in all phases of our game: opening, middle, and endgame. However, due to its characteristics, it is most often used in the opening, as it guarantees that we can move a block to the next higher level ahead of our opponent. It is characterized by aligning three blocks that cannot be stopped by dominating from below.

The Feedback maneuver has five phases: planning, investment, creation, launch, and recovery.

Strategic Concepts in XIMA

Significance of Initiative and Threats

It means setting the pace of actions in the game by capturing the opponent's attention through the threat of a constant ascension plan.

Regarding the importance of initiative, Capablanca states: quote "The initiative, when other conditions are equal, is an advantage, and therefore it is crucial to maintain it for as long as possible." quote

The threat is the fundamental weapon of the initiative, so we must strive to transform a simple threat into a double or multiple threat, making it difficult for the opponent to defend.

Remember that the objective of the game is to reach the top (XIMA) before the other player. Therefore, the player who first manages to bring at least two blocks, including a special block, to the third floor has a higher chance of reaching square G7. To achieve this, one must first bring at least three blocks to the second floor before the opponent.

Controlling the second floor before the opponent significantly increases our chances of success. From the initial phase of the game, the Opening, "the best moves from both sides are always subordinated to the struggle for the second floor." It is important to understand the most significant block structures established in this area, as this will decisively impact the strategy to be followed in the Middlegame.

The Importance of Central Squares and Upper Diagonals

When ascending quickly to reach the top, the central squares and upper diagonals are the primary "fast" ascension paths. During the opening, when fighting to reach the second floor before the opponent, these paths require minimal effort:

  • Doubling the column in the outer ring.
  • Supporting a block through the inner square.

If the position of the stepping block is not threatened, doubling the block on the outside is unnecessary. Likewise, there is no need to worry about other potential ascension paths the stepping block might generate.

The main advantage of corner squares and upper diagonals is their ability to save multiple turns simultaneously if managed wisely. Moreover, completely dominating a diagonal could force the opponent to sacrifice all their blocks, leaving them with no hope of stopping your victory.

Conversely, a significant disadvantage is that relying on a single ascension path makes it easy for the opponent to block it. If the planned structure is not solidified in time, a single move by the opponent could disrupt multiple turns, causing losses in space, time, and material.

Block Positioning and Double Attack

Blocks positioned in columns or doubled blocks provide a clear positional advantage. However, without a short-term ascension plan, this positioning can lead to a loss of time and material, as it hinders the movement of lower blocks and limits action points on the board. For this reason, it is not advisable to stack more than three blocks on the same square, especially in the inner ring, as they can be dominated easily.

Double Attack

Strategy and tactics are cornerstones in the development of a XIMA player. One of the most important tactical elements is the double attack—attacking two or more of the opponent’s blocks or important squares simultaneously with one of our blocks. This simultaneous attack can also target a weak point.

In the Middlegame, double attacks are most prominent due to the presence of fully developed blocks. While less common in the Opening (where blocks are not yet fully developed) or in the Endgame (where there are fewer blocks), the concept of the double attack still applies in these phases.

A double attack can only occur if there is a weakly defended block or square. Without such conditions, the attack cannot succeed. It is crucial to analyze and exploit the tactical theme of the double attack.

The double attack is a common tactical tool that often leads to effective and elegant finishes. Players should:

  • Acquire the technique to exploit double attacks.
  • Develop the skill to provoke these situations, using intuition.
  • Coordinate their blocks while ensuring their own forces are activated in harmony.

Glossary of terms

Incorporate
To take a block that is outside and place it on the board.
Align
The action of placing two blocks on the board so that they are in the same horizontal row, vertical column, or diagonal.
Cardinal
When two or more of your own blocks can dominate each other in the next move.
Semicardinal
When two or more of your own blocks can dominate each other, but some of them cannot dominate the others.
Dominate
The action of placing a block on top of another or others, preventing the lower blocks from moving.
Capture
When dominating a column of blocks, if there are opposing blocks in the minority, they are removed from the board without the possibility of reuse, except for the special block.
Block
When one or several blocks prevent the movement of other blocks trying to move to a specific square and are located on different levels of the board.
Pin
The action of being unable to move a particular block because it endangers a more important block, structure, or position.
Reserve
To keep at least one or several blocks for the final phase of the game to take advantage of a more significant opportunity.
Manage
To use resources effectively and organize a suitable technical plan to ensure victory.
Stack
To place one or several of your own blocks on top of each other in order to use a ladder to advance them to a higher level at all costs.
Divert
To make the opponent use more moves to achieve their goal.
Simplify
To sacrifice your own blocks in exchange for removing or immobilizing the opponent's blocks as well.
Threat
A potential danger if a specific action is taken.
Counterattack
To regain the initiative and do so as quickly as possible.
Create Ladder
To place a block in one of the interior squares.
Isolate
To obstruct coordination between two or more blocks.
Free
To allow dominated blocks to regain the possibility of movement.
Group
To place blocks together in squares or positions next to, above, or below each other, or as close as possible.
Synchronize
To create groups of blocks connected in such a way that they can coordinate in a few moves.
Recover
The action of bringing a common block to the top of the board to exchange it for the special block.
Sacrifice
A move where a piece or position is given up in exchange for gaining tactical advantage or superior positional compensation.
Occupy
To physically conquer a specific position before the opponent.
Press
To insist on the possibility of a threat.
Force
To make the opponent have no choice but to make a single move.
XIMA
The name of the board game of science with and without chance for two, four, or more participants, where each player has 10 blocks on a traditional three-dimensional pyramid-shaped board with 169 squares.
Cimist
A new word not found in the dictionary, meaning: a person who plays, studies, or loves the game of XIMA.
Pyramid
The name given to the XIMA board where the games are played, with its highest point being the main objective of the game.
Blocks
The name for the movable structures or forces that enter and move across the board.
Special
The block responsible for reaching the top and winning the game; it is the only block that can re-enter the game once captured.
Games of Chance
Games that depend on luck to develop, where winning or losing is not influenced by how well one prepares.
Games without Chance
Characterized by equal conditions for both players, where victory depends on the player’s preparation and not on luck.
Steps
The number of squares a block moves on the board (each new square is one more step).
Move
When more than one step is taken in a straight line and in the same direction from the initial square. (A move can consist of between 2 and 12 steps).
Interior Squares
Squares located next to the upper floors.
Interior Columns and Rows
Sets of interior squares next to each other, either horizontally or vertically, covering an entire face of the pyramid on any floor.
Inner Rings
Sets of rows and columns of interior squares interwoven with each other, surrounding the next upper floor of the pyramid.
Band
A set of interior and exterior columns or rows covering an entire face of the pyramid on the first, second, and third floors.
Floor
A set of interconnected bands surrounding the next upper floor of the pyramid.
Step Block
A block that allows the passage of others to a higher floor and is itself not capable of moving up on its own.
Middle Center
Refers to all squares comprising the second floor of the pyramid.
Top Center
Refers to all squares comprising the third floor.
Game
The human instinct to reflect one’s personality, both physically and mentally, for pleasure and fun.
Tower
A structure of blocks from the same or different players stacked on top of each other on the same square.
Natural Ladder
The space or square that a block can occupy, serving as a step for another block to advance to a higher floor in a straight line.
Artificial Ladder
The space or square that a block can occupy, serving as a step for another block to advance to a higher floor, but in two turns or moves, requiring it to be dominated first and then moved up in the next turn.
